# Public Bilingual Literature Search Practice

Use this reference when the task requires a current Chinese- and English-language literature base.
Treat every platform as a changing access path: verify it live before depending on its coverage,
query syntax, or download behavior.

## Translate the question into search families

Do not search only the paper's working title. Build terms for:

- political phenomenon and institutional setting;
- focal concepts and common synonyms;
- candidate mechanisms and rival explanations;
- outcome and population;
- country, region, policy, or historical period;
- empirical design and measurement terms; and
- adjacent disciplines that may use different language for the same process.

Build the Chinese terms independently. Record established Chinese disciplinary vocabulary,
alternative translations, abbreviations, policy names, and administrative terminology.

## Public English-language source stack

Use several complementary routes:

- OpenAlex for broad works, authors, venues, concepts, dates, and citation links:
  https://openalex.org/ and https://docs.openalex.org/
- Crossref for DOI and publisher metadata: https://www.crossref.org/documentation/retrieve-metadata/rest-api/
- Semantic Scholar for discovery and citation relations: https://www.semanticscholar.org/product/api
- DOAJ for open-access journals: https://doaj.org/
- discipline repositories and working-paper series such as SSRN, SocArXiv, OSF, NBER, and institutional
  repositories;
- journal, publisher, author, research-center, and replication-repository pages; and
- backward references and forward citations from the most relevant sources.

No single index has complete coverage. Cross-check important bibliographic and version facts against the
publisher, working-paper series, DOI record, or author page.

## Public Chinese-language source stack

Use multiple public routes:

- National Center for Philosophy and Social Sciences Documentation: https://www.ncpssd.cn/
- official CSSCI information from Nanjing University: https://cssrac.nju.edu.cn/
- public journal and university repositories;
- journal, publisher, author, think-tank, and research-center pages;
- public metadata pages from commercial indexes when accessible without login; and
- references and citing works in Chinese articles, dissertations, reviews, and policy-research reports.

Chinese journal metadata may be publicly discoverable when full text is not. Mark that distinction and
look for an author manuscript, institutional copy, conference version, or other lawful public text.

## Frontier and citation-chasing sequence

1. Search the current year and previous five years using multiple term families.
2. Identify recent reviews, syntheses, special issues, and field-defining recent articles.
3. Search backward for theoretical anchors, original measures, and design precedents.
4. Search forward for replications, critiques, extensions, and later published versions.
5. Search key authors and research groups for differently titled or in-process work.
6. Repeat searches using terms learned from the sources themselves.

Stop because searches are saturated around the research problem, not because an arbitrary paper count
has been reached.

## Retrieval and verification

For every source that may affect the synthesis:

- verify title, authors, year, venue or series, DOI or stable URL, and version;
- obtain the most complete lawful public text available;
- distinguish working paper, accepted manuscript, online-first, and version of record;
- retain page, section, table, figure, or appendix locators for important claims;
- record whether only metadata or an abstract was available; and
- link replication materials when they illuminate measures, code, or design choices.

Never treat a search-engine snippet, generated summary, citation count, or database label as the paper's evidence.

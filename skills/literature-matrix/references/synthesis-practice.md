# Literature Synthesis Practice

Use this reference after relevant sources have been retrieved and read. The aim is to reconstruct a
research conversation and revise the current project, not to summarize papers sequentially.

## Reconstruct each consequential study

Capture, in notes or an optional matrix:

- question and claimed contribution;
- concepts, theoretical process, and scope;
- main rival explanations;
- population, cases, period, and data;
- measurement choices;
- estimand or empirical target;
- design and identifying assumptions;
- main findings, magnitude, and uncertainty;
- diagnostics, contrary evidence, and limitations;
- source version and exact evidence locators; and
- relevance to the current project.

Use fields only when they help comparison. Do not force qualitative theoretical work or descriptive
research into an inappropriate causal framework.

## Diagnose disagreements

When studies disagree, ask whether the source is:

- different concepts hidden under the same label;
- different theories or scope conditions;
- different populations, cases, or periods;
- incompatible measures;
- different estimands or comparisons;
- changes in treatment implementation or institutions;
- sampling, attrition, or missing-data differences;
- estimation and uncertainty choices; or
- genuine unexplained conflict.

Do not average away these differences under a generic “mixed evidence” label.

## Write the synthesis around debates

Organize the memo by the intellectual problems that matter to the research question. For each section:

1. state the contested or cumulative claim;
2. compare the strongest relevant evidence;
3. explain what accounts for agreement or disagreement;
4. judge what is established and uncertain; and
5. state what follows for the current problem, theory, measurement, or design.

End with a revised contribution position. The contribution should name the specific belief, mechanism,
scope condition, measure, comparison, or finding the project could change.

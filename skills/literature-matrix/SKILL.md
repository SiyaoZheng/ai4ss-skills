# Literature Matrix

Conduct a substantive review of the scholarship bearing on the research problem. Search, retrieval,
close reading, source verification, comparison, and synthesis all belong to the task; bibliographic
metadata alone cannot establish the state of a literature.

## Literature synthesis

Write a **source-grounded literature synthesis memo** or working review that explains:

1. how the relevant conversation is structured;
2. the main theories, mechanisms, concepts, and scope conditions;
3. the principal empirical strategies and measures;
4. what the strongest studies find;
5. where findings or interpretations conflict and why;
6. which disagreements arise from theory, cases, measurement, design, estimand, or period;
7. what is established, uncertain, and genuinely open;
8. where the current project could contribute; and
9. how the literature changes the question, hypotheses, design, or interpretation.

An optional matrix may preserve source-level evidence, but a matrix without synthesis is incomplete.

## Access and Source Rules

Use public sources and do not assume institutional subscriptions or private database sessions.
Combine public scholarly indexes, journal and publisher pages, repositories, working-paper series,
author manuscripts, replication archives, and backward/forward citation chasing. Never bypass a
paywall.

Discovery metadata and abstracts can establish that a work exists, but they are not enough to
verify a substantive claim. For important claims, read the full accessible paper, manuscript,
appendix, or replication documentation and retain a page, section, table, figure, or other locator.

### English-language discovery

Use live public services such as OpenAlex, Crossref, Semantic Scholar, discipline repositories,
working-paper sites, journal pages, author pages, and cited-reference networks. Search concepts,
mechanisms, outcomes, cases, and method-specific terminology rather than one literal keyword string.

### Chinese-language discovery

Search in Chinese using field vocabulary and alternative translations. Use public access paths such
as the National Center for Philosophy and Social Sciences Documentation, official CSSCI metadata,
public journal and university repositories, journal sites, author pages, and citation trails.
Do not treat translated English queries as an adequate Chinese search strategy.

## Search Coverage

For the current frontier, search the current year and preceding five years, including online-first
articles and credible working papers. Then chase citations without a date limit to recover classic,
field-defining, and directly antecedent work.

Distinguish preprints, working papers, accepted manuscripts, online-first articles, and final
publications. Link related versions and let the newest verified version lead while preserving the
history when changes matter.

## Search, reading, and synthesis

### 1. Frame the search problem

Translate the research question into concept families, mechanisms, rival explanations, cases,
outcomes, and design terms in both Chinese and English. Identify adjacent literatures that may use
different labels for the same process.

### 2. Discover broadly, then chase deeply

Run multiple search strata, locate recent reviews and field anchors, and follow references backward
and citations forward. Search author networks and replication packages when they reveal unpublished
or differently titled work. Record enough of the search logic to update it later. Keep those notes
separate from the substantive review unless they are needed to explain its coverage.

### 3. Verify and read

Verify title, authors, date, venue or series, DOI or stable URL, and version. Read the portions needed
to establish theory, design, measures, evidence, and limitations. Do not copy an abstract's framing
as an independent assessment.

### 4. Compare studies as research

For each consequential source, reconstruct the question, theoretical claim, scope, data, design,
estimand or target, findings, uncertainty, and acknowledged limitations. Judge authority jointly
from venue or series, version status, substantive and methodological credibility, field position,
and relevance. Citation counts and index labels are signals, not verdicts.

### 5. Synthesize and revise

Organize the memo around debates and explanatory differences, not one-paper-at-a-time summaries.
Identify why studies disagree and what evidence could resolve the disagreement. End by revising the
current project's problem, theory, hypotheses, measures, design, or claimed contribution.

## Evidentiary standards

- Never invent citations, DOIs, publication status, quotations, or findings.
- Cite the source that directly supports each important claim.
- Separate what authors claim from what their design establishes.
- Preserve contradictory, null, and boundary-condition evidence.
- State when only metadata or an abstract was accessible.
- Do not declare a consensus from a small convenient sample of papers.
- Search both Chinese and English whenever the substantive question spans both conversations.

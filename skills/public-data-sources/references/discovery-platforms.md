# Public Research-Data Repository Search

Use public repositories to find replication packages, archived datasets, codebooks, and research
objects. Repository search is discovery, not a substitute for judging whether a dataset measures the
right construct, population, place, or period.

## Useful public starting points

| Platform | Where to search | Especially useful for | Research cautions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zenodo | https://zenodo.org/ | Open data, code, supplements, and versioned research objects | Inspect version, license, authorship, file list, and relation to any published paper. |
| DataCite Commons | https://commons.datacite.org/ | DOI-based discovery across repositories | A DOI record is metadata; follow it to the producing repository and documentation. |
| Harvard Dataverse and other Dataverse installations | https://dataverse.harvard.edu/ | Social-science replication packages and deposited datasets | Check dataset version, restrictions, terms, codebook, and whether files are truly public. |
| Figshare | https://figshare.com/ | Public datasets and journal supplements | Open the full record; verify authorship, license, version, and relation to the associated publication. |
| Dryad | https://datadryad.org/ | Published datasets linked to articles | Public metadata does not guarantee public files; follow the DOI and inspect the record. |
| OpenAIRE | https://explore.openaire.eu/ | Cross-repository discovery and publication-data links | It is an aggregator; confirm important facts at the original repository. |
| re3data | https://www.re3data.org/ | Finding an appropriate domain repository | It identifies repositories rather than individual datasets. |
| OSF | https://osf.io/search/ | Public projects, preprints, registrations, data, and code | Projects may be incomplete or have mixed public and restricted components; inspect the actual files. |

Access conditions can change. Consult the current record and repository terms before relying on them.

## Search as a research task

- Search the political concept, institutional setting, population, period, measure, and likely project
  or author names—not only the paper's title.
- Compare results across more than one repository because disciplinary and institutional coverage
  differs.
- Follow dataset records to related papers, codebooks, instruments, questionnaires, code, and prior
  versions.
- Treat a metadata-only record as a lead. Treat data as acquired only after opening or downloading the
  lawful public source and inspecting what it contains.
- Preserve the query, landing page, version, date of access, and original files needed to verify the
  source later.

Repositories that normally require login, application, restricted-use approval, or interactive terms
may still be excellent scholarly sources. Describe their access honestly; do not call restricted
materials public or attempt to bypass the restriction.
